Reflecting on: Moulthrop, S. (2003) ‘You Say You Want a Revolution? Hypertext and the Laws of Media (1991)’ (Blog 2 - Week 2)

Stuart Moulthrop’s essay explores the evolution of hypertext, its revolutionary potential, and its digital era effects. He shows how hypertext develops from Vannevar Bush’s concept of the Memex, and how it led towards the development of digital text as we understand it today. Moulthrop also explores what happens when hypertext is pushed to its limit, using Marshall McLuhan’s theory that every medium, when pushes to its limits, reverses into its opposite. He also continues to question whether hypertext as an interactive and open medium will join the pattern of radio and television, which started as interactive but later became corporate-controlled and hierarchical, so in essence he questions whether hypertext would remain a tool of free interaction or become a controlled system. How does this speak to the internet today? The early web was an open space where users could contribute freely and share knowledge or information. For instance, when Wikipedia was developed in 2001, anyone could contribute and edit articles. But as years went by, the internet followed the pattern of radio and television becoming a controlled system that is dominated by big corporate companies such as Google, Meta, Amazon etc that is driven by algorithms, data collection and advertising. For instance social media platforms such as Tiktok, Instagram and Facebook controls interactions by dictating what we see using algorithms. While this may be a good thing to some, the disadvantages is that it reduces the ability to explore other things freely. We have come to notice that a web is now a marketplace and most people rely on on it to market their businesses or companies using advertisements.

    An Analysis of UI and UX on the Property24 Website

    https://www.property24.com/for-sale

    In the current digital era, websites efficiency is mostly determined on how well they balance between usability and visual attractiveness. One of the top online real estate marketplaces in South Africa Property24, offers a case studies for analyzing application of UI and UX design. Even though the website is effective providing a user experience that is visual pleasing and intuitive, it has its cons when it comes to its universal accessibility.

    Interface design refers to how users interact with websites, and the user interface is emphasized by features like visual hierarchy, responsiveness, layout, and navigation with that in mind Property24’s user interface has been well designed. With the homepages noticeable search-box, users will be able to start searching for properties based on price, type, and location which is essential to users finding properties based on a simplified searching tool. To provide a clear and uncomplicated interface, the website also uses understandable typefaces, a consistent color scheme, and enough white space.

    Property24’s user interface is another thing that makes it good as the website allows access on a laptop, tablet, or smartphone, and the layout works well on all of them, so users have a smooth experience no matter what device they use. There are high-resolution pictures, price tags, and short details of each property listing, which makes it easy for users to quickly decide if the property is right for them.

    However, the UI is not without certain shortcomings. Some listing pages have too much information on them, making them look cluttered and a little overwhelming appearance.

    An Analysis of UI and UX on the AutoTrader Website

    https://www.autotrader.co.za/cars-for-sale

    However, the website does have some drawbacks, as noticed by the general layout advertising banners on some listings pages can make them visually overwhelming, as these components could divert users’ attention from their main objectives. Furthermore, the added filtering feature sometimes breaks while navigating, requiring users to re-enter their search parameters.

    Research: Internet, Society and Design Justice (Blog 6 - Week 9)

    The readings by Spyros Kasapis, "Internet Access is a Fundamepntal Right" (2020), and Claire Daniolou, "The Need for Global Internet Connectivity" (2020), address the urgent need for digital inclusion. Drawing from these perspectives and reflecting on the interview with Nanjira Sambuli, this response reflects on how digital design choices have a tendency to reinforce inequality and how a Design Justice perspective can yield more equitable solutions.

    From a Design Justice viewpoint, when many more population of people are excluded from digital platforms, it’s not just a technological gap but rather it becomes a systemic injustice. In fact, this digital infrastructure privileges the wealthy, urbanized, Western communities while silencing the voices of rural and global South. Kasapis calls us to reimagine internet governance with the question: who gets to determine the rules of the web? This short question throws politics into the works. Design Justice challenges us to engage communities most affected by design decisions directly in determining them which would include decisions about internet regulation, data policies, and access infrastructure.

    Daniolou highlights the economic, educational, and social benefits of being connected to the internet, particularly in rural communities. Examples among others include the GoGlobal, which shows how broadband access could uplift such regions suffering from the "rural penalty". Importantly, she also stresses that access alone is not enough — without technological literacy, the potential benefits of being connected cannot be maximized.

    As much as Daniolou stresses growth through connectivity, there is a danger of techno-solutionism: the belief that technology alone can fix systemic inequality. Design Justice warns us of this tendency. Simply bringing in technology without considering power dynamics, language access, local culture, or community agency risks replicating the same inequalities it seeks to solve.

    Besides this, Daniolou also touched on cultural erasure and misinformation, which are not mere side effects. In fact, they refer to the need for community-based, culturally-sensitive digital technologies that protect and empower diverse identities, instead of forcing global homogenization.

    In the interview with Nanjira Sambuli-digital equality advocate at the World Wide Web Foundation, they talk about how design practices mostly reinforce existing power structures unless intentionally disrupted. She calls for Design Justice, which involves redistributing power within design processes and enable marginalized communities to take on leadership in determining technologies, spaces, and systems.

    This directly connects to both Kasapis and Daniolou article that it's not just about access; it's also about whose interests are given priority, whose opinions are heard, and who controls the internet's design.

    Internet Access is a Fundamental Right (Blog 7 - Week 10)

    Introduction and Aim of the text:

    Spyros Kasapis’s article argues for the recognition of internet access as a fundamental human right with particular reference to the socio-economic impacts of the COVID-19 pandemic. His primary goal is to reimagine digital access—not as luxury or mere convenience—but as a necessary prerequisite for full participation in modern life, especially in the digital economy, education, and public discourse. The unstated assumption of the article is that the digital divide increases existing inequalities and that universal access to the internet is necessary for democratic participation and human dignity.

    Methodology:

    Kasapis begins with a reference to Salvador Allende's philosophical view of happiness conceived as a human right and argues that access to the internet, like education, food, and healthcare, is presently required for the exercising of that right. This argument places the internet not simply as infrastructure, but also as a social determinant of wellbeing. He substantiates his arguments by law: citing the judgment of the Indian Supreme Court that internet access is a fundamental right under free speech and privacy. This brief legal nod is intended to lend institutional authority to the argument, although the article lacks an in-depth legal or comparative analysis of international frameworks.

    Step-by-Step Breakdown and Critical Analysis

    1. Pandemic as a Revealer of Inequality

      2. Kasapis sees the pandemic in the light of a “spotlight” to inequalities, like the digital divide. Social distancing made everyday life digitalized: work at home, learning without having to go to school, health services online —and exposed how many people were left behind. His claim that “almost half of the world’s population does not have access” serves as a powerful indictment of the current global infrastructure.

      Critical Reflection: While persuasive, this section would benefit from quantitative accuracy. For instance, in 2020 ITU reports, some 37% of the global population had never used the Internet, a rather astounding but more nuanced figure than “half.” The argument however really can be made stronger by including some discussion of access differences amongst regions, for example, sub-Saharan Africa or rural Asia.

    2. Digital Inequality as a New Form of Exclusion

      3. Kasapis suggests that we are witnessing the emergence of digital inequality as a distinct form of social exclusion. An instance he uses is the explosion in Amazon sales and the wealth of Jeff Bezos during the pandemic in contrast to the billions that were excluded from participating in the digital economy.

      Critical Reflection: This particular section makes a strong ethical case but lacks concrete economic data. For example, how many of the unemployed were digitally excluded? What are the actual productivity gains enjoyed by digital firms? Such omissions might diminish the analytical depth, but serve a provocative moral function.

    3. Is Internet Access a Right or a Means to Rights?

      4. Kasapis acknowledges the philosophical tension between seeing internet access as a standalone right versus a facilitator of other rights. He avoids deciding in one direction or the other, although it appears he perhaps leans a little more to the former interpretation.

      Critical Reflection: This is the article’s most philosophically rich moment. Yet, he misses an opportunity to refer to literature on digital rights. The referencing of academic sources would help ground Kasapis's statement in academic discourse.

    4. Power and Governance in the Digital Space

      5. Kasapis ends with questions about whether governments should guarantee universal access and who should make rules about governance of the internet.

      Critical Reflection: These matters start very important debates on platform governance and state surveillance but do not really advance any road maps to putting concerns into concrete policies.

    Connecting to Additional Research: Technocolonialism and the Geopolitics of Access

    Kasapis's call for equitable access gains relevance when juxtaposed with Giorgos Verdi's article on technocolonialism. Verdi outlines how AI superpowers shape global dependency through digital infrastructure. In contrast, Kasapis proposes digital sovereignty based on universal access and democracy.

    Avenues for Future Research

    • Empirical study of the effects of digital exclusion

    • Comparisons of national policies

    • UX/UI practices for inclusivity

    • Governance alternatives to Big Tech

[online] Institute for Internet and Just Society. Available at: https://www.internetjustsociety.org/the-road-to-techno-colonialism [Accessed 01 May 2025].

    Critical Reflection: Making a Feminist Internet in Africa (Blog 8 - Week 11)

    The excerpt from Making a Feminist Internet in Africa presents a compelling case for reimagining the ways African women and LGBTIQA+ persons engage with, shape, and get shaped by digital spaces. What stuck with me the most about this reading was that the internet is not merely a tool of access but a contested space of power, identity, and survival. It seeks to change the mainstream view of African technology use as something purely functional and views digital engagement as inherently political and personal, especially for marginalized groups. As a digital arts student, I’m interested in how the internet becomes a site for both oppression and liberation. The reading resounded with concepts I had encountered in intersectional feminist theory-that visibility in digital space is also about resistance. As described here, the internet is merely continuing the realities of inequality found offline-racism, homophobia, sexism, or ableism-yet it is also the net that can be used as a tool to counter such inequalities by way of creativity, storytelling, or joy. The citing of body positivity, queer fashion, and makeup tutorials for gay boys as examples of "resistance made visible" makes me think that digital content truly holds the potential to accelerate cultural change.

    The part that resonated most was the recognition that many African voices remain missing online—not due to a lack of stories, but due to structural inequalities in access, infrastructure, and visibility. This comes close to describing my own personal experiences of how dominant online storytelling tends to happen from the West and fails to capture the complexities of African experience and identities. Having aspirations of working in UI/UX design, this reminds me even more about whose realities and experiences are put front and center while designing platforms and interfaces.

    The reading calls us to "create more room for African women online," is not a poetic invitation but a serious proposition to disrupt the status quo. It makes me look at my work not just as technical or artistic but as political. When we are designing systems, we must ask: who gets to feel safe here? Who gets to play, to be joyful, to be visible? A feminist internet, as imagined here, is not just an inclusive space- it is a joyful, pleasurable, and radically empathetic one. It asks us to center those most often marginalized and excluded, not as an afterthought but as an important foundation for building a better Internet.

    Reference:

    How our code matters in the context of decolonialism (Blog 9 - Week 12)

    Working with HTML and CSS to build my sci-fi-themed website got me thinking more deeply about how the very structure and design of a website can illustrate larger systems of power and knowledge — especially after reading the Decolonial AI: Decolonial Theory as Sociotechnical Foresight in Artificial Intelligence piece by Mohamed. In this reading, the authors share how in the context of AI coloniality continues to determine who gets to create knowledge and who gets to be represented in digital spaces. In my own projects, I have come to realize that aesthetic choices in language (sticking primarily to English) and familiar Western design frameworks (i.e., grids, logos, visuals) can play a role in excluding other cultural expressions and ways of engaging in technology.

    This awareness has prompted me to be more thoughtful in the themes, languages, and aesthetics I choose. For example, moving forward, I’m eager to play around with Afrofuturist design elements that truly capture local and culturally significant perspectives in my sci-fi projects—rather than just copying the Westernised sci-fi styles.

    At the present time, I have designed and styled my website, using HTML and CSS to create the structure and visual design. My design considerations have been focused on the layout or footprint of the website, the typography, and maintaining a cohesive visual experience between pages. One of the next steps is to add JavaScript and dynamic aspects, such as a responsive hamburger menu, interactive elements, and a light/dark theme toggle.

    While these abilities may be something we take for granted, the Decolonial AI reading encourages me to reflect on: For whom are these interactions really for? How do I ensure that they are designed for users from various backgrounds, especially users who are on a mobile device or have limited internet access? This relates to the discussion in the paper regarding structural decolonisation, which means not only designing for functionality or aesthetics, but also questioning what values and assumptions are built into the coding of our interaction design.

    I believe that the addition of JavaScript will make my interaction design more fluid by allowing the website to be more intuitive, responsive, and user-friendly. But I also want to be thoughtful about it—asking how each interaction can help with broader goals of accessibility, representation, and inclusion, especially for communities often marginalized in tech.

    Reference:

    What does an ethical internet look like to you? (Blog 10 - Week 13)

    An ethical Internet prioritizes human well-being, equity, accountibility, and causing no or limited harm. An ethical Internet is based on principles of inclusivity, privacy, transparency, and sustainability. New technologies should serve people rather than enrich them. Based on an article about Ethical Design and other research, this is what an ethical Internet should include:

    1. Inclusive and Bias-Free Design

    The internet has to reach the entire population regardless of race, gender, ability, or socioeconomic status. The soap dispenser example that could not recognize darker skin tones illustrates how unrecognized biases in design can enforce exclusion. In particular, elements of an ethical internet would include:

    • Diverse representation: in images, language, and user testing so stereotypes are not reinforced (e.g., only showing boys playing with a certain toy).

    • Universal accessibility: that ensures websites and internet applications meet the WCAG (Web Content Accessibility Guidelines) so people who may be differently able do not simply get marginalized.

    • Cultural sensitivity: Avoiding metaphors or symbols that might insult or exclude certain groups (such as skin-tone options for emojis).

    2. Privacy and Data Sovereignty

    An ethical Internet is conscious of user privacy and affirms people's right to control their own data. The article identifies and suggests that our personal data, whether the individuals realize this or not, is usually exploited for profit, leading to the creation of surveillance capitalism. Ethical practice include:

    • Minimal data collection: Only gathering what's necessary

    • Transparent consent: Users should understand everything they are consenting to and avoid dark patterns (e.g., hidden opt-out provisions).

    • Decentralization: Technologies such as blockchain or federated platforms can help free users from the grip of monopolistic corporations

    3. Transparency and Accountability

    Tech companies need to be held accountable for the algorithmic and moderation practices. Ethical design includes:

    • Explainable AI: Users should be able to understand why algorithms recommend content to them (e.g., Facebook news feed or YouTube recommendations).

    • Tackling misinformation: Platforms must focus on getting users accurate information over maximising engagement (such as Twitter's fact-checking system).

    • Ethically persuading: Not using manipulative UX that causes mental harm (e.g., infinite scrolling, alerts that create addiction).

    4. Mental Health and Well-Being

    There is much evidence surrounding social media's role in anxiety, depression, as well as polarization. Below are solutions:

    • Design for downtime: Features like Instagram's "You're all caught up" or screen-time trackers.

    • Ethical advertising: Refuse to engage in predatory targeting (e.g., targeted advertising for gambling to vulnerable audiences)

    Conclusion

    An ethical internet is not a utopian ideal, but it does provide a framework to hold designers, corporations, and policymakers accountable. It also provides a challenge to ensure that technology aligns with human rights. As the article states, ethical design means "doing good and not harming anyone," doing so intentionally or unintentionally. Engaging with what the ethics of stages or principles means can lead to an internet that enables rather than exploits, that connects rather than divides, and that sustains rather than exhausts.
