The Intersection of the Internet, Geopolitics, and Digital Coloniality: A Case Study of Google Maps Renaming the Gulf of Mexico

Introduction

The internet is influenced by geopolitical power structures, corporate decisions and historical inequalities. The most recent action by Google Maps to rename the "Gulf of Mexico" as the "Gulf of America" for US users (BBC, 2025) shows how digital spaces reinforce forms of geopolitical dominance and digital coloniality —a modern form of control where powerful nations and corporations dictate digital representations (Ragnedda & Ruiu, 2017). This essay critically examines this event from the perspectives of digital inequalities (Lutz, 2019; Robinson et al., 2015) and social capital (Ragnedda & Ruiu, 2017), showing how geopolitical narratives are enabled by tech companies.

The Event: Google Maps and the Renaming Controversy

In February 2025, Google Maps changed the name of the Gulf of Mexico to the Gulf of America for American users, following an executive order by President Donald Trump (BBC, 2025). It was defended that this change was framed as justification by Google's compliance with the guidelines from actual government sources, especially the U.S. Geographic Names Information System (GNIS), which had updated the name to reflect Trump's commitment to "restore names honoring American Greatness" (BBC, 2025). This unilateral decision gained more significance because the Gulf of Mexico is shared among three nations: the United States, Mexico, and Cuba. The renaming effectively erased Mexico's historical and geographical connection to this body of water in digital representations seen by U.S. users, while international users saw both labels: "Gulf of Mexico (Gulf of America)" (Reuters, 2025). This differential naming practice created fractured digital reality, where the same geographical feature is represented differently according to the geographical location of the user.

As a result, this sparked immediate diplomatic backlash from Mexico which argued that the U.S. had no legal right to rename a shared body of water under international law.

The Three Levels of Digital Divide (Ragnedda & Ruiu, 2017)

Ragnedda & Ruiu's (2017) framework of the three-level digital divide provides a critical framework for understanding the Gulf renaming controversy. Their model elaborates on three levels of digital inequality:

  1. First-level divide (access) - Differences in who has access to technologies. While this has traditionally referred to physical access to devices and connectivity, in this case study it manifests in who has the power to define digital representations of geography.

  2. Second-level divide (skills & usage) - Differences in the way people use the internet. In this case we see it with the different groups of users (U.S. vs. international) also experiencing different digital realities because of corporate decisions made in how to present their content.

  3. Third-level divide (outcomes) - The ways digital engagement translates into something beneficial or harmful in the real world. This is evident in how the renaming affects international relations and perceptions of sovereignty.

This case study deals particularly with the third-level digital divide, where digital platforms actually serve to reinforce offline power imbalances. The renaming of the Gulf goes on to prove that U.S. political straining dictates global digital representations, even against international objections, emphasizing what Robin et al. (2015) describe as the "stratification of digital spaces" (Ragnedda and Ruiu, 2017, p. 8). The selective use of the new name gives rise to what can be termed "digital territoriality" wherein control over digital representations becomes another extension of geopolitical power. This brings into focus the issue put forth by Lutz (2019) that digital inequalities not only pertain to access but also hinge upon who controls the narratives and representation within digital spaces. The Gulf renaming case exemplifies the very way through which digital platforms such as the internet become agents in what Ragnedda & Ruiu (2017) describe as symbolic domination in the digital space, in which the control of naming and representation becomes a form of soft power.

Digital Coloniality and Social Capital

The concept of digital coloniality that is explored based on the sources means the way dominant countries and corporations take control over the digital infrastructures enforcing their cultural and political standards globally(Robinson et al., 2015) In the case of Google renaming the Gulf based on U.S. government orders while ignoring Mexico's protest of the term, shows how digital platforms act as instruments of soft power. This follows what Lutz (2019) calls the “algorithmic control” (Lutz, 2019, p. 143) of geopolitical narratives, where corporate decisions regarding digital representational can lead to actual, diplomatic impact in the real world. This case shows the geographical politics of digital realms, where the control of the digital representation is the new battleground in which national power can be exercised.

Ragnedda & Ruiu (2017) present the argument that social capital (networks, trust, and influence) is critical to inequalities that occur in the digital context. Some of the main dynamics that we could observe in this case are:

  • The U.S government used its political capital to force a change of name, and such an example shows how state actors can use digital platforms to impose geopolitical narratives.

  • The diplomatic protest by Mexico was marginalized in the digital platform, a task that depicted as unequal social capital in the construction of digital narratives. It is an interpretation of what Robinson et al. (2015) call the "power relations" (Robinson et al., 2015, p. 578) of digital platforms.

This case shows how digital platforms have become what Ragnedda & Ruiu (2017) might call capital conversion machines, where political capital can be converted into digital forms that can be used to support geopolitical narratives. These differential naming practices generate the algorithmic sorting in digital spaces, as users of the same digital platforms perceive reality differently due to geographical location and political priorities of platform owners.

Analysis: Geopolitics and Corporate Power in Digital Spaces

  1. Corporate Compliance with State Power

    2. The move by Google demonstrates corporate deference toward state power, even in the cases when it is challenged on internationally. This is reminiscent of the colonial processes of the past in which territorial dominance was implemented through the creation of maps (Robinson et al., 2015). This is mirrored in the digital where sites such as Google Maps act as the agents of geographic truth in the modern day. The quickness of Google to effect the change of name in response to the executive order is an exemplification of what Lutz (2019) refers to as algorithmic responsiveness of technology platforms towards state power. According to Robinson et al. (2015), the reality of only a few corporations being in charge of digital mapping results introduces points of weakness in which geopolitical narratives can be easily modified through digital representations.

  2. Differential Visibility and Digital Sovereignty

    3. This selective renaming, in which the name Gulf of America is used only with the U.S. users and the name Gulf of Mexico is preserved with the Mexican users, displays a geopolitical division of the digital space. This aligns with Lutz's (2019) argument that digital inequalities are shaped by offline hierarchies. When Google tailors geographic terms according to national borders, the company enforces a disjointed digital environment in which the truth varies according to location. Such a habit results in the so-called digital parallel realities, as stated by Ragnedda & Ruiu (2017) where users in various countries get access to diverse variants of geographical truth.

Conclusion

The renaming of the Gulf of Mexico controversy shows that digital spaces such as Google Maps can turn into tools of geopolitical power enforcing digital coloniality through algorithmic decisions. This case exposes the impact of tech companies recreating real-world inequalities in digital spaces by prioritizing the U.S. political discourse over Mexican sovereignty. This selective renaming to American users generates parallel digital realities, enforcing what Ragnedda & Ruiu (2017) call the third-level digital divide- where online representations directly impact offline power relations. This case calls for the need to contest corporate-managed digital infrastructures that normalize geopolitical dominance that naturalize geopolitical supremacy under the guise of neutral platforms.
