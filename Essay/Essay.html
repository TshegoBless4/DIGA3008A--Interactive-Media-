Essay (One)

UI&UX website Analysis

South African Website offering services: Takealot.com Case Study

In the ever-evolving landscape of digital experiences, the ethical roles of UX/UI designers have become more crucial than ever. With individuals engaging with thousands of digital interfaces, designers' decisions have a direct impact on their behavior, expectations, and even values. This case study explores the design of South Africa’s leading e-commerce platform, Takealot.com, through the lens of ethical UX/UI design, using readings by Juned Ghanchi’s Ethical Design: Why Is It Critical for UX Designers? and Aparna K S Ethical UX Design and User Trust: Why it Matters? This essay will analyse the usability of the website, its accessibility and ethical designing of Takealot.com, while reflecting on the responsibilities of designers in shaping digital cultures.

Takealot.com is the leading ecommerce retailer in South Africa and one of the largest, most innovative ecommerce retailers on the African continent. It offers great customer services to South Africans from shopping all the way to delivery. It has a user-friendly interface, with intuitive navigation, it offers different categories and filter systems, visual presentations of the items people buy, all which allow for a quick and efficient shopping. The website includes features that allow customers to have an account personalization, recommendations, real-time order tracking and secure payment processes. The homepage design favours promotions and curated content across different departments, while the product pages include high-resolution images accompanied by the seller’s name, ratings, reviews. Product description and information. Takealot.com also offers a responsive mobile experience through its downloadable app and a mobile-optimized website to support access on different devices. Functionally, the website appears faultless and effective. However, when it is viewed through the lens of ethical design critically, some of the design choices need to be critically observed and examined.

Ethical Design as a UX Imperative

Aparna’s article Ethical UX Design and User Trust: Why it Matters? Calls attention to the need for ethical intention behind every UX decision. Aparna defines ethical design as one that supports user autonomy, informed consent, trust, and respect and encourages designers to reflect they are nudging users toward healthy, transparent decisions or manipulating them for the sake of business goals.

Similarly, Juned Ghanchi’s Ethical Design: Why Is It Critical for UX Designers? highlights the risks of bias, manipulation, and harm in digital products. Ghanchi categorizes ethical design into dimensions like usability, privacy, influence, sustainability, and societal impact. His argument reinforces that ethical design is not aesthetics or usability, but rather it's about being deliberately responsible to ensure that designs do not exclude or mislead users. With these arguments, we can analyse Takelot.com not just on how well it looks or works, but rather on whether it aligns with ethical UX principles.

Usability and Accessibility

In terms of usability, Takealot.com performs well. The website utilizes well- understood icons, clear product filters, a proper checkout process, and is quick to load. These, according to Ghanchi, serve what he calls a designer’s ‘’moral duty’’ towards functionality and reduce cognitive load. The site's responsiveness is part of accessibility with its support for mobile users and its adaption to different screen sizes.

However there are areas where accessibility could be improved. For instance, Takealot.com website does not provide complete screen reader support and has limited customizable visual features like contrast toggle or font size changes. Minor things like these are what Ghanchi refers to as small decisions that go towards ethical design in being attentive to individual needs, e.g light/dark mode feature or a better visual comfort setting. Takealot.com could be improved with more integration of such alternatives to accommodate visually impaired consumers.

Ethical Considerations of Persuasion and Influence

One of the more morally subtle aspects of Takealot.com's interface design is its use of persuasive UI patterns. The website uses urgency tactics such as "Only 2 Left in Stock" or "Limited Time Deal" which creates pressure to consumers to purchase items impulsively. Aparna critiques such dark patterns for prioritizing business outcomes over users’ well-being, and labels them unethical when they manipulate rather than inform.

Another crucial ethical issue is algorithm-based recommendation engines that often push forward products based on user activity. While this personalization improves the shopping experience, it raises ethical concerns about data usage and behavioural manipulation. Are users being shown what they need—or merely what will generate the highest conversion? Ghanchi warns that the line between white-hat and black-hat persuasion is thin, and crossing it undermines user trust.

Takealot.com does provide some transparency in the sense that they allow users to modify their notification preferences, and they do state the use of cookies. However, the ability for users to choose how their data is used for product recommendations could reinforce ethical alignment.

Privacy and Data Ethics

Data privacy is a central UX ethical consideration. Ghanchi argues that ethical design must include restrictions on what user data is collected and how it is used. On Takealot.com, users are required to create an account and also have to provide certain personal details like addresses and bank cards. The site appears to encapsulate users' data with sufficiently secure encryption and adheres to the e-commerce protocols. However, there is little transparency regarding how long your data is stored, whether or not it is shared with third parties, or any practices for anonymization.

Ethical UX would encourage more empowerment of users, such as allowing users to have more control over what data is stored, for how long, and the option to delete accounts or data footprints. Aparna feels that designers must consider consent as an ongoing process and not a fixed checkbox, and Takealot.com should work towards making this philosophy a reality.

Cultural and Societal Considerations

Design does not exist in a vacuum; it reflects and influences cultural values. Ghanchi’s discussion of representation and bias highlights the importance of ensuring that digital content reflects diverse users. Takealot.com largely avoids overtly biased content, but it is not entirely inclusive neither. For instance, product images and promotional content could better reflect South Africa’s linguistic diversity as a country with 11 official languages, this could make the platform feel more inclusive and locally grounded.

Ethical design includes fostering mental well-being. Takealot's gamified promotions, daily flash sales, and push notifications work to foster overconsumption or decision fatigue. Ghanchi encourages designers to consider sustainability in creating experiences that do not overwhelm or exploit psychological motivation.

Designer Responsibility and Systemic Ethics

Aparna raises the question surrounding designing experiences that support a healthy, humane digital world. As a leading brand, Takealot.com holds influence over digital norms in South Africa. Its design decisions therefore ripple through consumer behaviours and expectations.

While most of its practices are e-commerce standard, one could consider whether “standard” really should also mean “ethical.” Ghanchi reminds us that UX designers need to fit not only the business goals but the needs of the global and local communities. This entails designing for equity, transparency, and the greater good, even when the design is not working toward short-term profits.

Conclusion

In analysing Takealot.com’s UX/UI design through the ethical frameworks provided by Aparna K S and Juned Ghanchi, it is clear that while the site is highly functional and user-centered on the surface, it has more profound ethical issues that must be addressed. The website’s reliance on persuasive design, few accessibility features and lack of adequate user data transparency are representative of vast difficulties in digital design industry. However, these gaps also happen to leave space for opportunities. As Aparna argues in the article, ethical design thus boils down to human-centered decision-making that empowers, respects autonomy of and develops trust in users. Ghanchi goes one step further by calling designers to think about the impact they leave on privacy, sustainability, and society at large.
